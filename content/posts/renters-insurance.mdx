A significant portion of renters don't have renters insurance. Most of them think it's expensive, not necessary, or covered by their landlord's policy. All three are wrong.

---

## What your landlord's insurance covers

Your landlord has property insurance on the building. It covers:
- The structure (walls, roof, floors)
- The landlord's appliances and fixtures
- The landlord's liability for injuries caused by building defects

It does not cover your furniture, electronics, clothing, or any personal property. If the apartment floods and your TV and laptop are destroyed, the landlord's insurance pays nothing to you.

---

## What renters insurance covers

**Personal property:** Your stuff. Furniture, electronics, clothing, jewelry (up to a limit), musical instruments, sporting equipment. If it's stolen, burned, or water-damaged, your policy pays to replace it.

Standard perils covered (ACV or replacement cost):
- Fire and smoke
- Theft (including items stolen from your car)
- Vandalism
- Water damage from burst pipes or appliance overflow (not flooding — that's separate)
- Wind and hail
- Weight of snow or ice

**Liability:** If someone is injured in your apartment — slips, falls, your dog bites a visitor — liability coverage pays for their medical bills and legal costs up to your policy limit. This can be $100,000 to $300,000. Without it, you pay out of pocket.

It also covers incidents you cause that damage others' property — accidentally starting a fire that spreads to other units, your overflowing washing machine damaging the apartment below.

**Additional living expenses (ALE):** If your unit becomes uninhabitable due to a covered event (fire, flood from another unit, etc.), ALE pays for you to stay somewhere else — hotel, short-term rental — while repairs happen. This is often overlooked but extremely valuable.

---

## What it doesn't cover

- **Flooding from outside** (rain, rivers, storm surge) — requires separate flood insurance
- **Earthquakes** — separate policy
- **Pest damage** (bedbugs, rodents)
- **High-value jewelry, art, or collectibles** above the standard sublimit — schedule these separately
- **Business property or equipment** above the personal property limit
- **Intentional acts**

---

## The actual cost

Renters insurance costs $12–25/month for most people. The primary variables:
- Your coverage limits
- Your deductible (higher deductible = lower premium)
- Your location and crime rates
- Whether you bundle with auto insurance (most companies offer significant discounts)

A $15,000 personal property policy with $100,000 liability at a $500 deductible is typically $15–20/month. That's $180–240/year.

Compare that to replacing a stolen MacBook ($1,500), a flooded apartment destroying your furniture ($3,000+), or a dog bite lawsuit ($30,000+).

---

## ACV vs. replacement cost value

**Actual Cash Value (ACV):** Pays you the depreciated value of the item. A 4-year-old laptop that cost $1,500 might be valued at $400 after depreciation. This is cheaper to insure.

**Replacement Cost Value (RCV):** Pays you what it costs to buy an equivalent new item today. The 4-year-old laptop gets replaced with a current equivalent at current prices. This is worth paying extra for.

---

## How to buy it

- Your auto insurance company likely sells renters insurance and will bundle-discount both
- Lemonade, Toggle, and other online-first companies offer quick, low-cost policies
- Get quotes from multiple sources — prices vary significantly
- Look at the policy details: coverage limits, deductible, replacement cost vs. ACV, covered perils

Most states have no waiting period — you can buy a policy today and it's active today.

---

## Quick reference

- **Landlord's policy covers the building, not your stuff**
- **What renters insurance covers:** personal property, liability, additional living expenses
- **Cost:** $12–25/month. Bundle with auto for more savings.
- **Get replacement cost value, not ACV** — the difference matters when you claim
- **Check your state:** some landlords can require renters insurance as a lease condition
- **Flood and earthquake are separate** — standard policy doesn't cover these
