Generics are not cheaper versions of drugs. They are the same drug. The FDA requires it. The price difference is not a quality difference — it's a patent and marketing difference.

---

## What the FDA actually requires

To get FDA approval, a generic drug must demonstrate **bioequivalence** to the brand-name original. This means:

- The same active ingredient
- The same dosage form (tablet, capsule, injection, etc.)
- The same route of administration
- The same strength
- The same conditions of use

The active ingredient must reach the bloodstream at the same rate and to the same extent (within a ±20% range that the FDA has determined to be clinically meaningless for virtually all drugs).

The FDA requires generic manufacturers to meet the same manufacturing standards as brand-name manufacturers. The factories are inspected by the same agency under the same rules.

---

## Why generics are cheaper

Brand-name drugs are expensive because of:
1. **Patent protection** — when a drug is approved, the company holds an exclusive right to sell it for ~20 years (including the time it spent in development). No competition = whatever price they want.
2. **Marketing costs** — direct-to-consumer advertising, doctor outreach programs, branded packaging, rep visits to medical offices
3. **R&D recoupment** — the manufacturer spent hundreds of millions developing and testing the drug

When the patent expires, generic manufacturers can produce the same drug without those costs. They didn't spend on R&D or advertising. Competition between multiple generic producers drives the price down further.

The molecule itself doesn't change. The pill does — generics may have different fillers, dyes, or coatings (called inactive ingredients or excipients). These are the only real differences. For the vast majority of drugs, these make no clinical difference.

---

## The exception: inactive ingredients

A small percentage of people are sensitive to specific fillers or dyes in generic formulations. Thyroid medications (levothyroxine in particular) are commonly cited as drugs where some patients report responding differently to different manufacturers — the FDA has studied this, and the evidence is mixed.

If you or your doctor has a specific clinical reason to believe a particular brand matters for your condition, that's a real conversation to have. But "I prefer the brand name" without a specific reason is paying for a logo.

---

## What "generic" looks like on the shelf

The generic usually has the drug's scientific name on the label rather than a brand name:
- Advil → ibuprofen
- Tylenol → acetaminophen
- Zoloft → sertraline
- Lipitor → atorvastatin
- Prilosec → omeprazole

The strength and dosage are the same. Ask your pharmacist if you're uncertain.

---

## When you might not have the choice

Some drugs are still under patent and have no generic available. In this case, the brand name is your only option — but check:
- **Manufacturer coupons:** Brand-name manufacturers frequently offer coupons or patient assistance programs that can bring the price low, sometimes lower than the generic with insurance
- **Therapeutic alternatives:** Your doctor may be able to prescribe a different drug in the same class that has a generic equivalent

---

## The cost difference is enormous

Generic drugs cost 80–85% less than their brand-name equivalents on average. On long-term medications, this compounds:

| Medication | Brand monthly cost | Generic monthly cost |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Atorvastatin (Lipitor) | ~$300 | ~$10–20 |
| Sertraline (Zoloft) | ~$200 | ~$10–15 |
| Omeprazole (Prilosec) | ~$30 (OTC) | ~$10 (OTC generic) |

If your doctor writes "brand necessary" or "DAW" (dispense as written) on a prescription, ask them why. Sometimes there's a clinical reason. Often it's habit or pharmaceutical rep influence.

---

## Quick reference

- **Generic = same active ingredient, same dose, same bioequivalence** — FDA requires it
- **Price difference = patent + marketing costs**, not quality
- **Inactive ingredients differ** — relevant for a small number of sensitive patients, not most
- **Ask for generic.** If the pharmacy doesn't automatically substitute, ask them to.
- **Drug's scientific name = its generic name.** Know the name of what you take, not just the brand.
